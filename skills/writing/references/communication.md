# Communication Writing — Emails, Social Media, Messages

Reference for general communication. Applies to all languages; language-specific references add on top.

---

## Emails

### Structure

- **50-125 words** for highest response rates. Every extra word adds friction.
- **Lead with the ask or the value.** Not "I hope this finds you well." Not "I wanted to reach out regarding..."
- **One email = one purpose.** Don't bundle three topics into one message.
- **End with a clear next step.** Not "Let me know your thoughts" (too vague). Try "Can we do Thursday at 2pm?" or "Reply with your preference."

### Tone calibration

| Context | Register |
|---------|----------|
| First contact, senior stakeholder, client | Formal, no contractions, Sie (German) |
| Known colleague, peer | Conversational, contractions OK, du (German) |
| Team-internal, Slack-to-email | Casual, fragments OK |

Never start with: "I hope this email finds you well", "Per my last email", "Just following up", "I wanted to touch base". These are spam signals.

### Personalization

Generic flattery fails. Specific context works:
- Bad: "I really admire what your company is doing."
- Good: "Saw your talk at JSConf about edge caching — we ran into the same invalidation problem."

Anchor in a role-specific challenge, recent company news, or shared experience.

### German email specifics

- Use "Sehr geehrte/r..." for first formal contact, "Hallo [Name]" for known contacts, "Hi [Name]" for casual.
- German business emails tolerate longer sentences and more formal structure than English.
- Sign-off: "Mit freundlichen Grüßen" (formal), "Viele Grüße" (semi-formal), "Beste Grüße" (casual professional), "LG" (very casual).
- Always include a proper Grußformel — ending without one is rude in German business culture.

## Social media posts (LinkedIn, etc.)

### What works

- **Proof over polish:** Case studies, experiments, firsthand lessons (1.8-2x higher engagement vs. generic advice)
- **Personal stories:** "Here's what I learned" beats "Here's what you should do"
- **Specific data:** Screenshots, metrics, real numbers
- **Opinions with conviction:** Bold positions outperform fence-sitting
- **Conversational tone:** Write like you're talking, not writing a press release

### What fails

- Polished, jargon-heavy corporate updates
- Recycled motivational advice ("Hard work pays off")
- Generic thought leadership without proof
- Engagement bait ("Agree? Comment below!")
- Posts that could have been written by anyone — no specific perspective
- Direct translation of American social media tropes ("I'm humbled to announce" → just announce the thing)

### Structure

- **Hook in line 1-2.** The preview determines whether people engage.
- **Short paragraphs.** One thought per paragraph. White space matters on mobile.
- **Conversational, not performative.** Write "I" not "one." Write "we screwed up" not "challenges were encountered."
- **End with substance, not a call to action.** "What's your experience?" is fine. "Like and share if you agree!" is not.

## General principles (all communication)

- **Match the reader's register.** Don't write up or down. Match their level.
- **Cut first, add later.** Most drafts are 30-50% too long.
- **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, rewrite.
- **One pass for content, one pass for tone.** Don't try to do both simultaneously.
